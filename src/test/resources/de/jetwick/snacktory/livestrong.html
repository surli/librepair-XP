How to Decrease the Rates of Obesity in Children

How to Decrease the Rates of Obesity in Children

Overview

Childhood obesity increases a young person's risk for developing early onset diabetes, heart disease and high blood pressure. In the United States, 10 percent of children ages 2 to 5 are obese, and 20 percent of children 6 to 11 are obese, according to 2008 data from the Centers for Disease Control and Prevention. With few exceptions, increases in childhood obesity are a statistical trend in the United States. While the obesity rate typically rises each year, it doesn't have to. You can take proactive steps to ensure your children are healthier and avoid becoming one of the statistics.

Step 1

Feed your child several smaller meals during the day. When your child is at school, it's hard to regulate lunch habits, but replacing breakfast and dinner with four or five smaller snacks keeps your child's metabolism up for natural calorie-burning and prevents them from becoming too hungry and over-indulging.

Step 2

Prepare meals rich in complex carbohydrates, including whole grains. Citrus fruits rich in vitamin C also promote healthy weight loss. Grains and fruits help burn off "visceral" fat. Visceral fat is the type of fat accumulated around the abdominal region, and it's where most obese children need to lose the most weight.

Step 3

Educate your child about portion control and healthy food choices. Your child may eat junk food at a friend's house or use his allowance for snacks. When you take the time to explain healthy eating to your child, he feels included in this important life decision. Dietary knowledge lets your child feel in control, taking ownership for his own choices.

Step 4

Discuss weight-loss goals with your child and verbally recognize her progress. Even though obesity is increasingly common, obese children often feel "different" and isolated. It's important to make your child feel accepted and supported with verbal encouragement.

Step 5

Talk with your doctor about your child's caloric needs. As children mature, the amount of food required for healthy growth and development varies from year to year. For example, elementary school children need 2,200 calories each day, while preschool kids usually need 1,600 or less, reports the Alabama Cooperative Extension System. Based on your child's age, weight and physical ability, your doctor may recommend even fewer calories to encourage weight loss.

Step 6

Play outdoor games with a lot of running for at least 60 minutes each day. Cardiovascular exercise, like running, swimming and cycling, burns off visceral fat better than any other form of exercise. Basketball, football and soccer make cardio exercise more fun. When trying to reduce obesity in children, you experience better success if the weight-loss plan doesn't feel like a chore.

Tips and Warnings

  • Check with your pediatrician before beginning any weight-loss program. She will be able to advise you as to how many calories your child requires and any exercise restrictions he may have, and she might be able to help with suggestions about keeping your child's self-esteem intact.
